## Key findings by category

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Content strategies that increase AI visibility">
    The GEO paper (Aggarwal et al., KDD 2024) tested specific content modifications against 10,000 queries and measured the effect on AI engine visibility scores. Three strategies stood out:

    - **Quotations from authoritative sources: +41% visibility** — The single most effective GEO strategy. Including direct quotes from experts, studies, or official sources produced the largest average visibility gain across all tested modifications.
    - **Statistics and data points: +33% visibility** — Specific numbers, percentages, dates, and measurements were the second most effective strategy.
    - **Citing sources with in-text references: +28% visibility** — Naming sources inline and linking to primary studies was the third most effective strategy.

    Critically, lower-ranked pages benefit disproportionately. Sites originally ranked 4th–5th saw up to **+115% visibility improvement** from citing sources — a larger gain than sites already ranking in the top 3.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Content length and depth">
    From SE Ranking's analysis of 2.3 million pages (Nov 2025):

    - Articles of **2,900+ words average 5.1 AI citations** vs. 3.2 citations for articles under 800 words
    - The relationship is not purely about word count — it reflects that longer content tends to cover a topic more thoroughly, include more evidence, and provide more quotable passages

    Implication: key product and documentation pages should aim for substantive depth, not thin coverage. Depth signals to AI agents that the content is authoritative.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Content structure and heading density">
    From SE Ranking (2025) and AirOps (2025):

    - **44.2% of ChatGPT citations come from the first 30% of page content** (Kevin Indig, Growth Memo, 2026 — 1.2M AI answers). The opening sections of a page are disproportionately important. Leading with the direct answer rather than context or preamble is not optional.
    - **120–180 words between headings = 70% more ChatGPT citations** than shorter sections (SE Ranking, 2025). Sections that are too short or too long both underperform. The optimal density gives AI agents a well-scoped, self-contained block of information.
    - **H2/H3 hierarchy = 2.8x more likely to earn citations** (AirOps, 2025). Consistent, semantic heading structure is a strong signal of content quality.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Freshness signals">
    From SE Ranking (2025) and Ahrefs (2025):

    - **Content updated within 3 months averages 6 citations vs. 3.9 for content 2+ years old** — a 54% difference driven by recency alone (SE Ranking, 2025 — 2.3M pages)
    - **AI assistants cite content 25.7% fresher than traditional organic search results** — meaning AI engines have a stronger recency preference than Google's ranking algorithm (Ahrefs, 2025 — 17M citations across 7 platforms)
    - **85% of AI Overview citations come from content published in the last 2 years** (Seer Interactive, 2025)

    Implication: maintaining visible `article:published_time` and `article:modified_time` meta tags, and publishing regular updates or a changelog, is a concrete citation signal — not just good practice.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="FAQ sections">
    From SE Ranking's 2.3M page dataset (Nov 2025):

    - **Pages with FAQ sections average 4.9 AI citations vs. 4.4 without** — an 11% lift from adding a FAQ section
    - The FAQ *content* matters more than FAQ schema markup — the structured text is what gets cited
    - Adding FAQPage JSON-LD schema alongside the HTML FAQ section provides both the citation signal and the structured data signal
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="AI referral traffic distribution">
    From Conductor's analysis of 13,700 domains and 3.3 billion sessions (Nov 2025):

    - **ChatGPT drives 87.4% of AI referral traffic** — it is the dominant AI discovery channel by a very wide margin
    - Perplexity, Claude, and other AI platforms account for the remaining 12.6% combined
    - Implication: optimizing for how ChatGPT cites and surfaces content is not a niche concern — it is the core of AEO strategy in 2025–2026
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## What these findings mean for your site

Three findings from the research have the most immediate, actionable implications:

**1. Answer first, always.** Because 44.2% of ChatGPT citations come from the first 30% of page content (Growth Memo, 2026), restructuring existing content to lead with the direct answer — before context, before company history, before caveats — has a higher expected ROI than any other single edit. This applies to every page, not just blog posts.

**2. Cite sources inline, especially if you don't rank #1.** The +115% visibility gain for lower-ranked pages from adding source citations (GEO paper, KDD 2024) means that smaller or newer sites have a larger opportunity than established players. Source citations are both a content quality signal and a credibility signal that AI engines respond to directly.

**3. Update content regularly and make it visible.** AI assistants cite content 25.7% fresher than traditional search (Ahrefs, 2025). Adding `article:modified_time` meta tags and a visible "Last updated" date is a low-effort change that directly influences how often AI engines consider your content current enough to cite.

<Info>
  Lower-ranked sites benefit the most — sites originally ranked 4th–5th saw up to +115% visibility improvement from citing sources (GEO paper, KDD 2024). AEO optimization is a leveling force, not just an incremental gain for already-dominant sites.
</Info>
